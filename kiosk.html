UX Design:
Winter/Spring 2021
Brantford
Interactive Kiosk
How might we bring awareness
to Brantford businesses and improve
the experience of finding what bus to take?

My Role: All stages of the project; Making the 3D kiosk and "Dishes of the Month" prototypes

Timeline: February 15, 2021 to May 17, 2021

Topic: Business and Tourism



The Problem: Improving the public’s knowledge about Brantford’s businesses and bus routes

How do you like to discover new restaurants and businesses? Maybe you use apps like Google maps or Yelp to see what is around you. Or maybe you like to travel to places to see what stores are in that area.

For a school project, my team was challenged to create something that would solve a problem in the city of Brantford. To help us find a problem to solve, we were given the strategic plan of Brantford’s Business Improvement Area (also known as the BIA)which consisted of different goals. One of their goals my group wanted to explore was to work with their members and community partners to attract and support doing business downtown. Based on our own experiences in Brantford, we also wanted to explore the problem of Brantford’s transit system being confusing for newcomers like students.

Our Goal

With these problems in mind, we set our goal to answer “How might we bring awareness to Brantford businesses and improve the experience of finding what bus to take?”

Bringing awareness to Brantford businesses:

  • Based on our group’s experiences, apps like Google maps and Yelp don’t provide the best way to discover new businesses. Both new and non-franchised businesses can be hidden under the more popular or franchised-restaurants, making them harder for the public to discover. These apps also aren’t the best way for business owners to connect with the public and share information to potential customers.
  • It wasn’t until we did some more thorough research of the businesses that were in Brantford that we discovered that we were unaware of many of Brantford’s businesses. We discovered many new restaurants and businesses through a Brantford Facebook page. Many of these were not displayed on Google maps and Yelp unless you scrolled a lot.

Improving the experience of finding what bus to take:

  • When we first moved to Brantford, our group members found it confusing to figure out which buses to take. We had to rely on the Brantford transit website as Google maps did not show the Brantford buses (However, this has changed now).
  • On the Brantford website, there is a helpful resource called OpenStreetMap which shows live updates of where all the Brantford buses currently are. However this resource only works on computers and not phones, which doesn’t make it very useful as not many people carry computers with them when taking the bus.
  • Improving the experience of finding which bus to take could encourage people to travel to and from downtown Brantford more often, allowing more people to shop and dine at downtown Brantford’s businesses.

User Research: Surveying other Laurier students

We conducted a survey with 13 Laurier students to see if other students had similar opinions about their awareness of businesses and buses. Due to the pandemic, our research was limited to surveying students that we knew.

Businesses:

  • We learned that as of now, most students rely on Google and physically walking past stores to discover local businesses.
  • The majority of our survey participants shared that they only knew 4 to 7 of Brantford’s local businesses that were not part of a franchise and that figuring out what different businesses and restaurants are selling can be slightly difficult.
  • 91% of them eat and shop at only 1 to 3 of the local businesses that they know about.

Buses:

  • Many survey participants expressed that figuring out bus routes in Brantford was difficult. On a scale of 1 to 5 (5 meaning very difficult), 50% answered 4, and 33% answered 5.
  • Some express that this is because the information is difficult to find, especially without access to a computer.
  • The majority of them rely on Brantford’s website and map apps to learn about transit.


Ideation

Based on the BIA’s goals and the challenges users were facing, we needed to think of something that could

  1. Increase the low number of businesses students are aware about and attract them to new, local businesses
  2. Work with the businesses; help them connect with the public
    • Allowing them to have more control of sharing their information to the public would be a good way to do this
  3. Make it easier to learn about what bus to take, especially when users are trying to find information outside without access to a computer

Our Solution

After brainstorming and sketching ideas together, we came up with an idea of having an interactive kiosk to promote businesses and allow users to easily access travel information like maps and bus routes.

When interacting with the screen, users could view a variety of information provided by the businesses. Business owners can upload promotions, menus, and more to intrigue potential customers, who can see these on the kiosk. Business owners can also take a picture of a featured product and submit it to be displayed on the kiosk’s “Dishes of the Month” and “Products of the Month” section.

The kiosk also provides users with information on the best way to go to a business so that if someone found an interacting place they wanted to shop or eat at, they can easily learn how to get there through the transit and map features. The kiosk can also be used to search for how to get to any address in Brantford. Lastly, the kiosk includes live bus updates provided through OpenStreepMap software, allowing users to know how long they need to wait for the next bus and where it currently is. These features will help address the difficulties that many have with learning about Brantford’s buses.

Besides working with businesses in the ways mentioned above, the kiosk also monitors, collects, and analyzes patterns and analytics. With such data, businesses can then gain information about what pictures/advertisements are the best at attracting users to learn more about the business on the kiosk, how many users actually shop and dine at the business after learning about it at the kiosk, and much more.

When no one is interacting with the screen, the kiosk will display advertisements for Brantford’s businesses. Given that many of the survey participants discovered new businesses by walking past them, by walking past these interactive kiosks, they could also see information about businesses and learn about them. The advertisements in Brantford are mainly posters, so having moving advertisements displayed on a kiosk, would be eye-catching to people walking past it.

Another Problem: COVID-19

However, because of the COVID-19 pandemic, we had to consider sanitation and concerns about the spread of COVID-19 through touch, so we created a kiosk which could minimize the amount of users who touch the screen. Our kiosk design features foot buttons and an automatic sanitizer mist to address this problem.

For the foot buttons, users can interact with the design in a hands-free method by using the physical buttons located at the bottom of the design, decreasing the need for one’s hands to touch the screen. Originally, we had the buttons on the ground in front of the screen (where the person is standing in the prototype), but we decided to move them to underneath the screen so that it would not block wheelchair users from using the kiosk. There is also an accessibility mode to lower the display so that wheelchair users and people who cannot reach very high can have an easier time using the kiosk. The kiosk also features voice assistance which can read text out loud to increase accessibility.

Even without concerns about spreading COVID-19, the foot buttons are beneficial. For example, if it’s winter and you’re wearing gloves, you don’t need to take off your gloves to use the kiosk. If you’ve just finished grocery shopping and want to figure out what bus to take but you’ve got grocery bags in both hands, the kiosk’s foot buttons will be extremely helpful as well.

For the automatic sanitizer mist, when the screen hasn’t been interacted with during a period of time, there will be a visual and audio countdown to indicate that the screen will be sanitized. However, if it is too difficult to implement this feature, it can be removed. Instead, the screen can be cleaned physically every once in a while.

As the fall term starts, our team is in talks to meet with the Brantford Economic Development and Tourism team to see if they are interested in making our solution a reality. We also have ideas of alternatives if the cost of this kiosk design is too expensive. These include having a normal kiosk instead, similar to the map kiosks at malls, or even a website alternative. Although these will not have the same amount of benefits as our kiosk with the foot buttons, no matter which option the city chooses, our design is an improvement to the resources people currently use to learn about Brantford’s businesses and transit as it centralizes the information into one easy-to-access resource. It also provides new ways for business owners to promote and share information about themselves, and it gives them more control over how their business is promoted, compared to existing resources like Google maps and food apps. By making it easier for people to travel to and from downtown Brantford and making it easier for them to learn about local shops and restaurants, our design will help businesses and the public’s support for them continue to grow.
